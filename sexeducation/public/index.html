The Sex Education in Hong Kong

by Liu Xiaoqian, Zhao Minzhu, Zhou Zhen

VIEW

Background

Many young people approaching adulthood are faced with a conflicting, negative, and confusing message about sexuality. Public discussion of sex is discouraged by laws or social norms in many Asian societies. But silence does not undermine the significance of sex education.

The World Health Organization believes sex education should have its place in schools.

According to the International Technical Guidance on Sexuality Education by WHO, proper sex education enables children and young people to develop:

  • Accurate and age-appropriate knowledge, attitudes and skills;

  • Positive values, including respect for human rights, gender equality and diversity;

  • Attitudes and skills that contribute to safe, healthy, positive relationships.


Sex Education in Hong Kong

In Hong Kong, the only sex education guideline issued by the government was "Guidelines on Sex Education in Schools" in 1997, which never been updated ever since. the 1997 Guidelines lay down five key sexuality concepts and the related topics to be covered in different stages of learning:

Human Development

Human sexuality, reproductive system & physiology, puberty, self-image and self-concept, sex identity and orientation and emotions.

Health and Behavior

Handling of sexual drives, bodily privacy, sexual habits and behavior, sexually transmitted diseases, contraception, unwanted pregnancies and personal body care.

Interpresonal Relationships

Basic values, personal skills, friendship, dating, love and infatuation, sexual harassment, abuse and violence, and incest.

Marrige and Family

The meaning of the family, interdependence of family members and relationships between the generations, family conflicts and their resolution, changing patterns within the family, and etc.

Society and Culture

Societal and cultural influences on sex, gender roles, sex and the mass media, sex and the law, sex, morality and ethics.

Implementation

The latest available report on schools’ implementation of sex education in Hong Kong was published by the Red Ribbon Centre of the Department of Health in 2013, which shows an inadequate understanding of its implementationat junior level of secondary schools in Hong Kong.

Why is it important?


Too many young people receive confusing and conflicting information about relationships and sex, as they make the transition from childhood to adulthood (UNESCO, 2018).

When children grow up, they need to learn and adapt to the physiological and psychological changes at different stages of development with appropriate guidance (Student Health Service Department of Health, HKSAR, 2010).

A lack of high-quality sexuality and relationship education may leave children and young people vulnerable to harmful sexual behaviours and sexual exploitation.


To Prevent Sexual Harassment


According to a survey of sexual harassment among university students in Hong Kong from the Equal Opportunity Commission (EOC) in 2019, many interviewees redeem the insufficient or lack of “sexuality education” as the root cause of sexual harassment at universities. It also mentions that students may not know how to treat people of different gender with respect without proper sexuality education.


To Prevent Teenage Preganancy


Young people without sufficient contraceptive knowledge may have higher rates of the unplanned pregnancy, which can also have serious educational, health and financial consequences.

As suggested in the Report on Youth Sexuality Study 2016 by the Family Planning Association of Hong Kong (FPAHK), the percentage of 18-27-year-old females who had been pregnant rose from 10% in 2011 to 14% in 2016.

Sexuality education for juveniles was added to the Article 40 of the Law of the Peoples Republic of China on the Protection of Minors in the third session of the 13th National People's Congress (NPC) in May 2020. Schools and kindergartens are required to offer age-appropriate sexual education to juveniles to improve their awareness and ability to protect themselves from sexual assault and harassment.


However, sex education is not mandatory for schools in Hong Kong and some schools may not have time for that due to the intense curriculum (Young Wang, 2017). Although the Government has issued guidelines on sexuality education and relevant curriculum guides for schools' reference in implementing sexuality education, there is still room for improvement.

The Age of Consent around the world


The Age of Consent is the legal age at which an individual is considered mature enough to consent to have sex. Sexual relations with someone under the Age of Consent are considered statutory rape, even (in some jurisdictions), if both partners are themselves younger than the Age of Consent.

The map shows the legal age of consent for sex around the world. In most countries, the age of sexual consent is between 14 and 20 years old, while the age of consent in a few countries is “after married”.
